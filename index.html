First, sound goes in the auditory cortex and the sounds making up part of speech take a special pathway to Broca’s area in the Inferior frontal guys and Wernicke’s area in the superior temporal gyrus.

Broca's Area of the brain is used for speech production and articulation.

Wernicke's Area of the brain is used for comprehension

Processing begins...

The brain breaks up the sentence into different parts and different regions in the brain recognize consonants and tone.

Semantics and syntax are handled by Wernicke’s area, which also handles speech, motion and face processing. This suggests that people who have developed these areas of the brain by speaking multiple languages are better at processing these tasks associated with the same area. Broca’s area is associated with comprehension and keeps track of certain words in short term memory.

What happens to the brain with learning of languages?

Multilingualism has been shown to correlate with neural growth, forming connections all around the brain for increased cognitive function and influence on perception, with the idea of linguistic relativity.

Neural Growth in the Brain

"When we learn a language, we create new neural pathways in our brain, which can lead to noticeable changes. The left hemisphere is generally believed to be the logical part of the brain and is where many of our language skills originate. However, a 2012 Swiss study observed that learning a foreign language later in life is associated with thickening of the cerebral cortex — a layer of neurons specifically responsible for memory, thought, consciousness and, of course, language. This increased thickness can lead to better memory and sharper thinking later in life." -Languajob

Specifically, the Dorsolateral Prefrontal Cortex is a part that shows growth in strength. This cortex plays a role in executive functions of the brain, meaning that it's growth makes the brain more healthy, complex, and actively engaged."

Linguistic Relativity

Linguistic relativity is the idea that language, while not dictating thought, has an influence on perception. Languages, with their different vocabularies and grammatical structures force us through particular perceptions.

Examples:
  1. Japanese contains more words to describe blues than English does, and Japanese speakers are able to distinuguish more shades than English speakers.
  2. Hebrew has gender markers while Finnish does not. Herbrew speaking children knew their own genders a year earlier than their Finnish counterparts
  3. Italian speakers would say "the lamp broke itself" while English speakers would say "they broke the lamp," indicating that English speakers search for the source to blame, rather that it being a mere occurance.

Benefits of Knowing Languages

  • Brain growth leading to a higher density of the gray matter that contains most of your brain's neurons and synapses

  • Better executive control--the set of cognitive skills based on limited cognitive resources for such functions as inhibition, switching attention, and working memory

  • Increased attention and verbal/spational abilites

  • Prevention against dementia and alzheimers

  • Improved decision-making skills: According to a study from the University of Chicago, bilinguals tend to make more rational decisions. Any language contains nuance and subtle implications in its vocabulary, and these biases can subconsciously influence your judgment. Bilinguals showed more confidence in their choices after thinking it over in the second language and seeing whether their initial conclusions still stand up.
